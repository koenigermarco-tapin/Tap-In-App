Purple Belt · Stripe 3

Most people negotiate like it's a zero-sum game.

They think: for me to win, you have to lose. So they fight for every inch. They hide information. They see compromise as defeat.

They're playing the wrong game.

The best negotiators don't split the pie. They make the pie bigger.

The Framework

Every negotiation ends in one of four places.

Win-Win
Both parties get what they need. Relationship strengthens.
Win-Lose
You win now, but damage the relationship for later.
Lose-Win
You give in to keep peace. Resentment builds.
Lose-Lose
Both suffer. Often happens when egos take over.

Win-Win isn't naive idealism. It's the only outcome that's sustainable.

The Insight

Positions vs. Interests: the key to everything.

This is the core insight from "Getting to Yes," the Harvard Negotiation Project's landmark work:

Position
What you SAY you want
Interest
WHY you want it
The Classic Example

Two people fight over an orange. They compromise: each gets half. But one wanted the peel for baking, the other wanted the juice. Both could have had 100% of what they needed.

Practice

Spot the interest behind the position.

Situation

Your colleague says: "I need this report by Friday—no exceptions."

What might be the underlying interest?

They want to assert authority over you
📅
They have a Monday presentation and need weekend prep time
😤
They're being inflexible for no reason
Exactly
The position is "Friday, no exceptions." The interest is likely prep time for a presentation. Once you know that, you might offer: "What if I get you the key data Thursday and the full report Monday morning?" Same interest met, different solution.
The Tool

The magic question: "Help me understand why."

When someone states a position, don't argue against it. Instead, get curious about what's driving it.

"Help me understand why that's important to you."

— Your new favorite phrase

This does three things: reveals their real interest, makes them feel heard, and shifts from adversarial to collaborative.

Variations: "What problem does that solve for you?" or "What happens if we can't do that?"

The Process

Four steps to Win-Win.

1
Separate people from problem
Attack the issue, not each other. Stay curious, not defensive.
2
Focus on interests, not positions
Ask "why" and "why not." Dig beneath the surface demand.
3
Generate options together
Brainstorm before deciding. Quantity over quality at first.
4
Use objective criteria
Base decisions on fair standards, not who's more powerful.
Deep Dive

Step 3 is where the magic happens.

Most negotiations fail because people jump to solutions before fully understanding the problem. Options expand the pie.

The Brainstorm Rules

• Separate inventing from deciding
• Go for quantity—wild ideas welcome
• Look for shared interests
• Ask: "What would make this easy for you to say yes?"

The question that unlocks options: "What if we...?"

Advanced

Know your BATNA.

BATNA = Best Alternative To Negotiated Agreement. It's what you'll do if this negotiation fails.

Why BATNA Matters

Your power in any negotiation comes from your ability to walk away. If you have no alternative, you have no leverage. Before any important conversation, know your BATNA.

Example: If your manager won't approve remote work, your BATNA might be accepting it, asking HR, or finding a new job. The stronger your BATNA, the more confident you can be.

Watch Out

Three traps that kill Win-Win.

🎭
Positional bargaining: Arguing over positions instead of exploring interests
Premature closure: Jumping to the first solution instead of generating options
🤐
Hiding information: Treating negotiation as combat instead of problem-solving

The antidote to all three: stay curious, share openly, take time.

The Shift

Win-Win is a mindset, not a tactic.

You can't fake it. If you're secretly trying to "win" while appearing collaborative, people sense it.

"Seek first to understand, then to be understood."

— Stephen Covey

The genuine belief: "There's probably a solution here that works for both of us. Let's find it."

This isn't being soft. It's being smart. Win-Win preserves relationships you'll need for the next negotiation.
