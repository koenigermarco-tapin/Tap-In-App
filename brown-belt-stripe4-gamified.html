Development ≠ Promotion
Development doesn't require title changes. Stretch projects, mentoring others, leading initiatives, representing the team—all create growth without promotion. The goal is capability, not hierarchy.

The 70-20-10 Learning Model.

Development doesn't primarily happen in classrooms. Research shows how people actually grow:

Where Learning Happens

70% — Challenging experiences (stretch assignments, new roles, leading projects)

20% — Relationships (mentoring, coaching, feedback, observation)

10% — Formal training (courses, workshops, reading)

Most organizations over-invest in the 10% and under-invest in the 70%.

Apply It

A high-potential team member asks you how to develop their strategic thinking. Using 70-20-10, what's your best first move?

Recommend books on strategy
Send them to a leadership program
Include them in your next strategy meeting
Set up coffee with a senior strategist
Experience First

Including them in a real strategy meeting (70%) creates more learning than books (10%). The meeting shows context, teaches the process, and builds confidence. Pair with debrief conversations (20%) after.

Three development relationships.

Each serves a different purpose in someone's growth:

Know the Difference

Coaching — Asking questions that help them find their own answers. Performance-focused. You don't need expertise in their area.

Mentoring — Sharing experience and guidance. Career-focused. You've walked the path they're on.

Sponsoring — Advocating for them in rooms they can't enter. Opportunity-focused. You use your influence on their behalf.

Scenario

A team member is qualified for a senior role but keeps getting passed over. What development relationship do they need most?

Coaching to improve their performance
Mentoring to navigate the political landscape
Sponsoring to advocate in leadership discussions
More training to build their skills
Sponsorship Gap

If they're qualified but overlooked, the issue isn't capability—it's visibility. They need sponsorship: someone who speaks up for them in talent reviews and puts their name forward for opportunities.

Delegation is development.

There are four delegation levels—most leaders only use two:

Four Levels

Level 1 — Do exactly as I say (new/critical)

Level 2 — Research and recommend (developing)

Level 3 — Decide and inform (trusted)

Level 4 — Fully own it (autonomous)

The goal is to move people up levels. What starts as Level 1 should become Level 4 over time.
