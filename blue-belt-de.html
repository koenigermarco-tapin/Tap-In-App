Welcome to The Plateau

In Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu, Blue Belt is where most people quit. You've learned the basics (White Belt), but now comes the grind—the messy middle where progress feels slow and the work gets uncomfortable.

Blue Belt in leadership is the same. You know yourself. Now you have to express yourself—especially when it's hard.

From Awareness to Action

White Belt was about knowing yourself. Blue Belt is about expressing yourself authentically—even when it creates tension.

Lencioni's second dysfunction is "Fear of Conflict." Teams that avoid conflict don't fail because they hate each other—they fail because they're too polite, too careful, too afraid to say what needs to be said.

Great teams debate fiercely and decide quickly. Mediocre teams avoid debate and let resentment build.

The BJJ Principle: Pressure Testing

In Jiu-Jitsu, Blue Belt is when you start rolling (sparring) regularly. Your techniques get tested under pressure. You learn what works, what doesn't, and where your gaps are.

It's uncomfortable. You get submitted. You make mistakes. But this is where growth happens—in the discomfort of real resistance.

Leadership conflict is the same. Healthy teams test ideas through debate. They challenge each other. They say the hard thing. They pressure-test decisions before committing.

"Comfort is the enemy of progress. Pressure reveals truth."

The Research

Amy Edmondson's research at Harvard found that the highest-performing teams report more mistakes—not because they make more errors, but because they're psychologically safe enough to discuss them openly.

Lencioni's research shows that teams with productive conflict make better decisions, implement faster, and have less politics than conflict-avoidant teams.

The goal isn't to create drama. It's to mine for conflict—to surface the disagreements that already exist and resolve them through honest debate.
