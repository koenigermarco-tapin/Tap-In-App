Lesson 1
Mining for Conflict
Start

üéØ What You'll Learn

  • How to surface hidden disagreements in teams
  • Techniques for "mining" conflict that's being avoided
  • When and how leaders should provoke productive debate

üìñ Core Concept

In most teams, the real conflicts don't happen in meetings‚Äîthey happen in parking lots, Slack DMs, and hallway conversations. As a leader, one of your primary jobs is to mine for conflict: deliberately surface the disagreements that people are avoiding.

This feels counterintuitive. Most leaders try to reduce conflict. But if you're not seeing any conflict in your meetings, you're not seeing honesty.

üîë Lencioni's Insight

"If everyone around the table agrees too quickly, it's probably not because you have perfect alignment‚Äîit's because someone isn't speaking up."

Leaders must actively extract the conflict that's lurking beneath the surface of artificial harmony.

‚õèÔ∏è Mining Techniques

1. Call Out the Silence

"We agreed on this too easily. What are we not saying?"

2. Assign Devil's Advocate

Explicitly ask someone to argue the opposite position: "Sarah, can you make the case against this decision?"

3. Name the Elephant

"I think there's tension around resource allocation that we're not discussing. Let's put it on the table."

4. Ask Specific People Directly

"Marcus, you've been quiet. What concerns do you have?" (Silence is often disagreement in disguise.)

5. Pause Before Decisions

"Before we finalize, I want to hear one concern from each person."

üìä Why Teams Avoid Conflict (Even When Leaders Ask)

People don't speak up even when directly asked because:

  • Social risk: "If I disagree, I'll look like a problem."
  • Futility belief: "The decision is already made anyway."
  • Retaliation fear: "Challenging the boss is career suicide."
  • Peer pressure: "Everyone else is agreeing, so maybe I'm wrong."

The leader's job: Make disagreement explicitly safe and expected.

ü•ã The BJJ Analogy

In sparring, if your partner isn't resisting, you're not learning. The same is true for team decisions. If nobody pushes back, you're not getting better ideas‚Äîyou're getting compliance.

A good leader, like a good training partner, invites resistance because that's where growth happens.

‚ö†Ô∏è Warning Signs You Need to Mine Deeper

  • Meetings end with "consensus" in under 15 minutes
  • Nobody asks clarifying questions
  • The same voices dominate while others stay quiet
  • People agree in meetings but express concerns privately afterward
  • Decisions get revisited or implementations stall

üí° Key Takeaways

  • Artificial harmony is the enemy of good decisions‚Äîleaders must mine for hidden conflict
  • Quick consensus is often a red flag, not a green light
  • Make disagreement safe by explicitly inviting it and modeling it yourself

‚ú® Practice Exercise

This week: In your next team meeting, use at least 2 mining techniques:

  1. "We agreed too quickly. What are we missing?"
  2. "Before we decide, I want one concern from each person."
  3. Assign someone to play devil's advocate

Track: Did new information emerge? Did the decision change?

Lesson 2
Real-Time Facilitation
Start

üéØ What You'll Learn

  • How to facilitate conflict in real-time during meetings
  • Interventions to keep conflict productive vs. destructive
  • Reading the room and adjusting your approach on the fly

üìñ Core Concept

Mining for conflict gets disagreement into the room. Real-time facilitation is what you do once it's there‚Äîhow you manage the conversation so it stays productive and doesn't spiral into personal attacks or shutdowns.

This is the hardest leadership skill to develop because it requires reading emotional cues, making split-second decisions, and intervening without controlling.

üîë The Facilitator's Balance

Too hands-off ‚Üí Conflict becomes destructive or dominates
Too controlling ‚Üí You shut down the very conflict you mined for

The sweet spot: Guide without directing. Create structure without stifling energy.

üõ†Ô∏è Real-Time Interventions

1. Redirect to Ideas, Not People

When: Conflict becomes personal ("You always..." or "That's because you...")
Say: "Let's focus on the idea, not the person. What specifically about the proposal concerns you?"

2. Equalize Airtime

When: One person dominates while others shut down
Say: "Jordan, hold that thought. I want to hear from the folks who haven't spoken yet. Alex, what's your take?"

3. Slow Down Escalation

When: Voices get louder, interruptions increase
Say: "This is important. Let's slow down so we can really hear each other. One person at a time."

4. Reframe Disagreement as Data

When: People seem uncomfortable with the conflict
Say: "This tension is good‚Äîit means we're exploring different perspectives. Keep going."

5. Call a Tactical Pause

When: Emotions are too hot to process information
Say: "Let's take 5 minutes. Come back ready to find a solution together."

üìä Reading the Room: What to Watch For

Body Language What It Means Your Move
Arms crossed, leaning back Defensiveness or disengagement Invite them back in: "I notice you've gone quiet. What's your read?"
Leaning forward, interrupting High energy, possibly escalating Slow it down: "One at a time. Let's make sure everyone is heard."
Looking at phones/laptops Checked out‚Äîconflict feels unsafe or futile Re-engage: "This matters. I need everyone present."
Eyes darting to you Looking for rescue or permission to speak Invite them: "You look like you have something to add. Go ahead."

ü•ã The BJJ Mindset: Staying Calm Under Pressure

In BJJ, when someone passes your guard or gets you in a bad position, panicking makes it worse. The same is true in facilitation.

When conflict escalates, your job is to stay regulated so you can think clearly. Use your own physiology as a guide: if your heart is racing, take a breath before intervening.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Facilitation Mistakes

  • Rescuing too quickly: Jumping in at the first sign of tension kills productive conflict
  • Forcing resolution: "Let's agree to disagree" avoids the real work
  • Taking sides: Even subtle agreement with one position undermines your neutrality
  • Talking too much: The facilitator should talk less than everyone else combined

üí° Key Takeaways

  • Facilitation is about guiding conflict, not controlling it
  • Watch body language to read when to intervene and how
  • Your own emotional regulation is your most important tool

‚ú® Practice Exercise

This week: During a team conflict, practice one of these interventions:

  1. Redirect to ideas: "Let's focus on the idea, not the person."
  2. Equalize airtime: "I want to hear from folks who haven't spoken."
  3. Reframe tension: "This tension is good‚Äîit means we care."

After: Reflect on what you observed. Did the intervention help? What would you do differently?
