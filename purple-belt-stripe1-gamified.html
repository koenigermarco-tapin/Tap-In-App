There are two types of conflict.

Productive conflict is about ideas. It's passionate debate about what's best for the team. It makes decisions better.

Destructive conflict is about people. It's personal attacks, politics, and ego. It tears teams apart.

"If everyone is thinking alike, then somebody isn't thinking."

— General George Patton

Scenario

Your team is about to make a decision you think is wrong. Everyone else seems to agree. The leader asks, "Any concerns?"

What do you do?

Stay quiet. It's not worth the conflict.
Say nothing, but bring it up privately later.
"Actually, I see a potential issue..."

The Right Move
Speaking up in the moment is productive conflict. You're challenging the idea, not the people. Staying silent lets bad decisions happen. Attacking makes it personal. And bringing it up privately later? That's politics—it creates back-channels and undermines trust.

The IDE Framework for productive conflict.

I
Intention
State your positive intent. "I want this to succeed, which is why..."
D
Data
Share what you see. Facts, not judgments. "I noticed that..."
E
Explore
Invite dialogue. "What am I missing?" or "Help me understand..."
Apply it

A colleague's project is behind schedule, and it's affecting your work. Using IDE, which response is best?

"Your delays are making me look bad to my stakeholders."
"No worries, just let me know when you're ready."
"I want us both to succeed here. I'm seeing the timeline slip, and it's impacting my deliverables. What's happening on your end?"
"I guess some people just work at different speeds."
IDE in Action
Option 3 uses all three elements: positive intention ("want us both to succeed"), data ("timeline slip"), and exploration ("what's happening"). The others are either attacking, avoiding, or passive-aggressive—all destructive patterns.
