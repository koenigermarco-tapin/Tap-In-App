The STOP Protocol.

When you feel the heat rising, remember:

S
Soften
Lower your voice. Slow your pace. Relax your body.
T
Thank
Find something to appreciate. "Thanks for being direct..."
O
Open
Ask a genuine question. Show curiosity about their view.
P
Pause
If needed, suggest a break. "Can we revisit this in 10 minutes?"
Scenario
Your manager (angry)

"I can't believe you sent that email without running it by me. You made the whole team look bad."

Using STOP, which response de-escalates best?

"I didn't have time. You were in meetings all day."
"I hear you—this is a problem. Can you help me understand what specifically landed wrong so I can fix it?"
"Sarah said it was fine to send."
"I thought I was being proactive. Isn't that what you wanted?"
STOP in Action
Option 2 uses Soften (acknowledging without defensiveness), Thank (implicit appreciation for the feedback), and Open (genuine question). Excuses, deflection, and defensiveness all escalate.

The most powerful de-escalation tool.

"Between stimulus and response there is a space. In that space is our power to choose our response."

— Viktor Frankl

Before people can hear you, they need to feel heard. Validation creates that space.

Validation isn't agreement. It's acknowledgment.

Powerful Phrases

"I can see this is really frustrating."

"That makes sense given what you're dealing with."

"I'd feel the same way in your position."

"I hear you—this matters to you."

Notice: none of these say "you're right." They say "I see you."

Colleague (frustrated)

"You always do this! You commit to things and then don't follow through. I'm tired of covering for you."

The word "always" is a sign they're flooded. Best response?

"That's not fair. I don't always do this."
"I can hear how frustrated you are. You've been picking up slack, and that's not fair to you. Can we talk about what specifically fell through?"
"I think you're overreacting a bit."
"You're right, I'm terrible. I'm sorry."
Validation + Redirect
Option 2 validates their frustration without agreeing to the "always." It acknowledges the impact, then redirects to specifics. Countering "always" triggers more defensiveness. Over-apologizing doesn't solve anything.

Sometimes the best move is to pause.

When the heat is past the point of no return, the wisest thing is to step away.

The Professional Pause

"I want to give this the attention it deserves. Can we take 10 minutes and come back?"

"I'm feeling reactive right now, and I want to respond thoughtfully. Can we continue this in an hour?"

Never: "I'm done with this conversation" or walking out without explanation.
