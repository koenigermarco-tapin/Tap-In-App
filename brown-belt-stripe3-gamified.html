You need to implement a new process. Leadership approves, but the team doesn't see why it's needed. First step?

Send an email announcing the change and timeline

Create Urgency First
Without urgency, people ask "why bother?" Show them the cost of staying the same before you explain the solution. Data, stories, and real consequences create the felt need to change.

Scenario

You're implementing a change. One influential team member is openly skeptical. Others are watching to see what you do. Best move?

Move forward—you can't wait for everyone
Meet with them privately: "Help me understand your concerns"
Work around them—focus on supporters
Remind them this decision has been made
Convert Skeptics, Don't Ignore Them
Influential skeptics can become your best allies—if you earn their buy-in. Meet privately, listen genuinely, and address real concerns. Often they see risks you've missed. If you convert them, others follow.

Quick wins create momentum.

People need to see that change is possible before they'll commit to it. Identify small, visible victories in the first 30-60 days.

Quick Win Criteria

Visible — People can see the improvement

Unambiguous — Clearly connected to the change

Early — Happens within weeks, not months

Celebrated — You make it a moment

Scenario

You're implementing a new project management system. The team is skeptical. Which quick win should you prioritize?

Get full executive dashboard live in 90 days
Eliminate one frustrating manual report in the first week
Train all 200 employees within the first month
Document all processes before making any changes
Why this works

Eliminating one frustrating report in week one shows immediate value. People feel heard. The change solves their problem, not just leadership's. Build from there.

Beyond Kotter: The ADKAR Model.

Individual change requires a personal journey. ADKAR focuses on what each person needs:

ADKAR Framework

Awareness — Why is change needed?

Desire — What's in it for me?

Knowledge — How do I do it?

Ability — Can I actually do it?

Reinforcement — Will this stick?

Quick Check

The Desire Gap

Most leaders excel at communicating awareness but fail at building desire. People understand the change intellectually but don't feel motivated to embrace it personally. This is where emotional intelligence matters.

When trying to build desire for change, where do you typically start?

Pain of staying Benefit of changing
Both Matter

Effective change leaders use both. Start with pain to create urgency, then paint a compelling vision of the benefits. People move away from pain AND toward pleasure.

Change fatigue is real. When everything is a priority, nothing is. Great change leaders sequence and pace their initiatives.

Apply It

Your organization has 5 change initiatives planned. Leadership wants to launch them all at once to "save time." Best response?

Agree — Let's move fast and get it done
Prioritize — Launch two now, sequence others
Delay — Wait until the team is less busy
Delegate — Give each initiative to a different team
Strategic Sequencing

Prioritizing and sequencing isn't slower — it's smarter. Two well-executed changes beat five half-done ones. Quick wins from early initiatives build appetite for later ones.

The emotional journey of change.

Everyone goes through predictable stages. Understanding this helps you support people at each phase.

The Change Curve

Shock — "This can't be happening"

Denial — "This won't affect me"

Frustration — "Why is this happening?"

Depression — "I can't do this"

Experiment — "Maybe I can try..."

Decision — "This could work"

Integration — "This is how we do things now"

Scenario

A team member says: "I've been doing it this way for 10 years. Why change now?" Which change curve stage are they in?

Shock
Denial
Frustration

Reading the Signs

This is denial — they're minimizing the change's impact on them. Don't argue facts. Instead, help them see personal relevance: "What if the old way isn't available in 6 months?"
