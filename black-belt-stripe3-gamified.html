The Feynman Technique.

Nobel physicist Richard Feynman had a simple test for true understanding:

4 Steps to Mastery

1. Choose a concept
Pick something you want to understand deeply

2. Teach it to a child
Explain it in simple terms, no jargon

3. Identify gaps
Where do you struggle to explain simply?

4. Review and simplify
Go back to source material, then try again

If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough.

The 3-part mini-lesson.

Simple Teaching Structure

1. The Hook
Start with why it matters. A problem, a question, a moment of recognition.

2. The Framework
Share the concept—keep it simple. One model, one acronym, one principle.

3. The Application
Give an example or ask them to apply it. Make it real.

You can teach almost anything in 5 minutes using this structure.

Apply it

A colleague is struggling with giving feedback. They ask for your advice. Using the 3-part structure, what's your best opening?

Start with the definition of good feedback
Start with a question: "What makes feedback hard for you?"
Jump straight into the SBIR framework
Share a time you gave bad feedback
Start With Their Problem
The best teaching starts with the learner's experience, not your framework. Asking what makes it hard creates relevance—now they want the solution. Then introduce SBIR as the answer to their specific challenge.

Meet them where they are.

Good teachers don't just share information—they build bridges from what the learner already knows.

Build incrementally

Three teaching mistakes to avoid.

What Doesn't Work

The data dump
Sharing everything you know instead of what they need

The rescue
Jumping in with answers before they've struggled enough

The assumption
Assuming they understood without checking

Teaching is about their learning, not your knowledge display.

Scenario

You're explaining GROW coaching to a peer. They nod along but look confused. What's your best move?

Repeat the explanation more slowly
Move on—they'll get it eventually
Ask: "What's your understanding so far?"
Provide more detail and examples
Check, Don't Assume
Asking them to explain back reveals exactly where they're stuck. More explanation doesn't help if you're explaining the wrong thing. Let them show you where to focus.
