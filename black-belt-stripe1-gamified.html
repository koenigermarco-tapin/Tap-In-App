The Results Paradox

When teams prioritize everything except outcomes ‚Ä¢ 25 XP

üéØ The Ultimate Dysfunction

You've conquered trust, conflict, commitment, and accountability. But here's the brutal truth: teams can have all four and still fail.

The paradox: Teams trust each other. They have healthy conflict. They commit. They hold each other accountable. But to what? Their own status, ego, career advancement‚Äînot the collective results that matter.

Lencioni's definition: "The tendency of members to care about something other than the collective goals of the group."

This lesson: why results are the hardest dysfunction to overcome, and what "results" actually means.

üö´ What Teams Prioritize Instead of Results

1. Individual Goals Over Team Goals

"I shipped my feature" (even though the product still doesn't work end-to-end)

  • Engineers optimize for technical elegance vs. customer value
  • Sales reps chase personal quota vs. sustainable customer relationships
  • Managers protect their team's metrics vs. company outcomes

2. Status and Ego

"I want to be seen as the expert" (even if the project fails)

  • Defending decisions to look smart rather than course-correcting
  • Hoarding information to maintain power
  • Refusing to admit mistakes because it damages reputation

3. Departmental Silos

"My department succeeded" (even though the company lost)

  • Marketing celebrates campaign metrics while sales tanks
  • Engineering ships on time with terrible UX (Product's problem)
  • Each team optimizes locally, system fails globally

4. Activity Over Outcomes

"We're working really hard" (but what did we accomplish?)

  • Measuring hours worked, meetings attended, tickets closed
  • Rewarding busyness instead of impact
  • Confusing effort with results

5. Career Safety

"Don't make me look bad" (even if it helps the company)

  • Avoiding risky but necessary decisions
  • Political maneuvering instead of truth-telling
  • Protecting turf instead of collaborating

Inattention to results: Each team optimized for their departmental scorecard instead of the collective outcome that would make or break the company.

  • Shared: Everyone on the team owns them, not just the leader

Lesson 2: Individual Achievement vs. Collective Results

The Individual Success Trap: When team members prioritize their own success metrics, recognition, and career advancement over collective outcomes.

Why Individuals Prioritize Personal Over Team Results

Performance Review Systems: Most organizations reward individual contributions, not team outcomes. If your review measures your output (not the team's), that's what you'll optimize for.

Recognition & Visibility: Credit goes to individuals, not teams. Standing out requires visible personal achievements.

Career Advancement: Promotions depend on what you did, not what your team accomplished. This creates incentive to shine individually.

Competitive Pressure: Limited spots, budgets, and opportunities create zero-sum thinking within teams.

What Individual-Over-Team Looks Like

Example 1 - The Data Hoarder: An analyst discovers insights but doesn't share them because they want to present the findings themselves for recognition.
Example 2 - The Glory Hunter: An engineer takes on high-visibility features and avoids unglamorous-but-critical infrastructure work the team needs.
Example 3 - The Scope Protector: A PM defends their roadmap against team needs because hitting their targets matters more than solving the right problems.

The Cost to Team Performance

Behavior Individual Benefit Team Cost
Hoarding information Appears more valuable Decisions made with incomplete data, duplication of effort
Avoiding hard problems Easier deliverables, looks productive Critical issues unaddressed, technical debt grows
Defending turf Control, clear ownership Silos, integration failures, customer experience suffers
Taking credit Recognition, promotions Resentment, others disengage from collaboration

Reorienting to Collective Results

1. Define Shared Success Metrics - What defines success for the team as a whole? Make it clear and measurable.

2. Tie Individual Performance to Team Outcomes - "You succeed when the team succeeds" must be more than rhetoric - build it into evaluations.

3. Celebrate Team Wins Publicly - Recognition events should highlight collective achievements, not just individual heroics.

4. Make Trade-Offs Explicit - When someone chooses personal glory over team needs, name it and address it.

Real Transformation: A product team moved from individual feature ownership to shared quarterly outcomes. Everyone's review depended 70% on team metrics (customer retention, NPS) and 30% on individual contribution. Result: Collaboration increased, feature silos disappeared, and team retention improved by 15%.

üìö Research: Collective Focus

Google's Project Aristotle: Found that psychological safety and shared purpose (not individual talent) predicted team performance. Teams where members subordinated individual goals to collective outcomes outperformed "superstar" teams by 40%.

Key insight: High-performing teams reward contribution to collective success, not individual heroics.

Lesson 3: Results Clarity - Defining and Measuring Outcomes

The Clarity Problem: Teams can't focus on results if they don't know what results mean. Vague goals ("increase engagement," "improve quality") lead to vague efforts and ambiguous outcomes.

Why Teams Avoid Defining Clear Results

Accountability Aversion: Specific results create accountability. If you define success clearly, failure becomes equally clear.

Measurement Difficulty: Some outcomes are genuinely hard to quantify (culture, innovation, long-term impact).

Political Safety: Vague goals let everyone claim success. Clear goals mean someone might fail publicly.

Short-Term Pressure: Defining meaningful results takes time. Teams under pressure settle for activity metrics instead.

Activity vs. Outcomes: The Critical Distinction

Activity (What You Do) Outcome (What Changes)
"Ship 5 new features" "Increase user retention by 10%"
"Run 20 sales calls" "Close $500K in new revenue"
"Conduct 10 customer interviews" "Identify and validate the top 3 customer pain points"
"Refactor authentication system" "Reduce login failures by 25%, improve security audit score to 90+"
"Hold weekly 1-on-1s" "Improve team engagement score from 6.5 to 8.0"

Activity metrics measure effort. Outcome metrics measure impact. Results-oriented teams track outcomes and use activities as a means to an end.

The SMART Results Framework (Applied Correctly)

SMART goals are overused but often misapplied. Here's how to use them for results clarity:

  • Specific: "Increase revenue" ‚Üí "Increase Q2 revenue from SMB customers by 15% ($300K ‚Üí $345K)"
  • Measurable: Can you objectively determine if you achieved it? No interpretation required.
  • Achievable: Stretch goal, but not fantasy. Based on data, not hope.
  • Relevant: Tied to broader team/company objectives. Avoid vanity metrics.
  • Time-Bound: "By end of Q2" not "soon" or "eventually."
Before (Vague): "Improve customer satisfaction this quarter."
After (Clear): "Increase NPS from 42 to 50+ by June 30th, with no detractor score above 15%."

Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

Lagging Indicators: Outcomes you care about (revenue, retention, customer satisfaction). These tell you if you succeeded but are slow to move.

Leading Indicators: Activities that predict lagging indicators (qualified demos, onboarding completion rate, support response time). These give you early signals and are faster to influence.

Best teams: Define clear lagging indicators (the results), then identify 2-3 leading indicators to track progress and make course corrections.

Lagging: Increase annual revenue by 20%
Leading:
  • Qualified demos per week (target: 15)
  • Demo-to-close conversion rate (target: 25%)
  • Average deal size (target: $50K)

Common Results Definition Mistakes

1. Too Many Metrics: Tracking 15 KPIs means tracking none effectively. Pick 1-3 that truly matter.

2. Metric Substitution: Measuring what's easy instead of what matters (e.g., tracking "features shipped" when retention is the real goal).

3. Sandbagging: Setting goals so low that achievement is guaranteed. This kills motivation and growth.

4. No Baseline: "Increase revenue" without knowing current state makes tracking impossible.

5. No Owner: Results without clear ownership become everyone's responsibility (meaning no one's).

üìö Research: Goal Clarity

Harvard Business School study: Teams with specific, measurable outcome goals outperformed teams with vague aspirational goals by 250% on average. Clarity of results definition was the single strongest predictor of execution success.

Key insight: Ambiguity is the enemy of achievement.
