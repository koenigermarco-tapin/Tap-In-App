The Results Paradox

When teams prioritize everything except outcomes ‚Ä¢ 25 XP

üéØ The Ultimate Dysfunction

You've conquered trust, conflict, commitment, and accountability. But here's the brutal truth: teams can have all four and still fail.

The paradox: Teams trust each other. They have healthy conflict. They commit. They hold each other accountable. But to what? Their own status, ego, career advancement‚Äînot the collective results that matter.

Lencioni's definition: "The tendency of members to care about something other than the collective goals of the group."

This lesson: why results are the hardest dysfunction to overcome, and what "results" actually means.

üö´ What Teams Prioritize Instead of Results

1. Individual Goals Over Team Goals

"I shipped my feature" (even though the product still doesn't work end-to-end)

  • Engineers optimize for technical elegance vs. customer value
  • Sales reps chase personal quota vs. sustainable customer relationships
  • Managers protect their team's metrics vs. company outcomes

2. Status and Ego

"I want to be seen as the expert" (even if the project fails)

  • Defending decisions to look smart rather than course-correcting
  • Hoarding information to maintain power
  • Refusing to admit mistakes because it damages reputation

3. Departmental Silos

"My department succeeded" (even though the company lost)

  • Marketing celebrates campaign metrics while sales tanks
  • Engineering ships on time with terrible UX (Product's problem)
  • Each team optimizes locally, system fails globally

4. Activity Over Outcomes

"We're working really hard" (but what did we accomplish?)

  • Measuring hours worked, meetings attended, tickets closed
  • Rewarding busyness instead of impact
  • Confusing effort with results

5. Career Safety

"Don't make me look bad" (even if it helps the company)

  • Avoiding risky but necessary decisions
  • Political maneuvering instead of truth-telling
  • Protecting turf instead of collaborating

Context: SaaS company launching new feature. Each team has goals:

  • Engineering: Ship by Q2 (hit it ‚úÖ)

Inattention to results: Each team optimized for their departmental scorecard instead of the collective outcome that would make or break the company.

üéØ What "Results" Actually Means

Results are:

  • Collective: Team/company outcomes, not individual achievements
  • Measurable: Specific numbers, not vague "doing well"
  • Time-bound: By when, with what target
  • Meaningful: They actually impact the mission/business, not vanity metrics
  • Shared: Everyone on the team owns them, not just the leader

Examples of real results:

  • ‚ùå "Improve customer satisfaction" ‚Üí ‚úÖ "NPS from 30 to 45 by Q3"
  • ‚ùå "Ship the redesign" ‚Üí ‚úÖ "Increase conversion rate from 2% to 3.5% with redesign by Aug 1"
  • ‚ùå "Work on retention" ‚Üí ‚úÖ "Reduce churn from 5% to 3% monthly by end of year"
  • ‚ùå "Be more efficient" ‚Üí ‚úÖ "Cut support ticket resolution time from 24h to 12h avg by Q2"

ü•ã The BJJ Parallel: Tournament Performance

You can have perfect technique in the training room. You can drill for hours. You can know every position. But when you step on the tournament mat, only one thing matters: did you win your matches?

Inattention to results in BJJ:

  • "I almost submitted him" = you lost
  • "I had better technique" = you lost on points
  • "I'm usually better than this" = today's result is what counts
  • "I train way more than them" = effort doesn't determine outcome

Results-focused approach: Training is important, technique matters, effort counts‚Äîbut they're all in service of the result: winning matches. Everything else is preparation.

Leadership parallel: Strategy sessions, team-building, process improvements‚Äîthey're all preparation. The result is what you deliver to customers, stakeholders, and the market.

üìö Research: Goal Clarity and Performance

Locke & Latham (Goal-Setting Theory): Teams with specific, challenging, collective goals outperform teams with vague or individual goals by 250%.

Google's Project Oxygen: #1 trait of high-performing teams: "Clear goals and roles." Not trust, not collaboration‚ÄîCLARITY on what success looks like.

McKinsey study: Only 23% of employees can name their company's top 3 priorities. 77% are working on things that may not matter.

Key insight: Most teams fail not because they're incompetent, but because they're optimizing for the wrong scoreboard.

üí™ Practice: Results Audit

For your team, answer honestly:

  1. What are your team's top 3 measurable results for this quarter? (If you can't name them in 10 seconds, that's the problem.)
  2. What % of your team's time goes toward those results vs. other work?
  3. If your team hit all its internal metrics but those 3 results failed, would you consider the quarter successful?
  4. Can you name the collective result that matters more than your individual performance?
Red flag: If you can articulate YOUR goals clearly but struggle to name the TEAM result that you're all chasing together, you're experiencing inattention to results.
