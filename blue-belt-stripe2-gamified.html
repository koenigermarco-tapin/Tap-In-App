Lesson 1
Disagree & Commit
Start

üéØ What You'll Learn

  • The Netflix principle of "Disagree & Commit"
  • How to separate debate from execution
  • Why full consensus is often the enemy of speed

üìñ Core Concept

Most teams get stuck in one of two traps:

  1. The Consensus Trap: Endless debate trying to get everyone to agree. Decisions take forever. Innovation stalls.
  2. The Dictator Trap: Leader decides unilaterally. Fast, but people don't buy in. Execution suffers.

"Disagree & Commit" offers a third way: Debate vigorously, decide clearly, then commit fully‚Äîeven if you disagreed with the decision.

üîë The Netflix Culture Principle

"You can disagree strongly during discussion, but once a decision is made, you commit as if it was your idea. This creates alignment without forcing fake consensus. The alternative? Passive-aggressive execution."

Supporting a decision you disagree with is leadership. Undermining it is sabotage.

ü•ã How It Works

Phase 1: Disagree (The Debate)

  • Everyone voices their real opinion
  • Disagreement is expected and encouraged
  • Data, perspectives, concerns all get aired
  • The goal is the best decision, not agreement

Phase 2: Commit (The Execution)

  • Decision gets made (by leader, vote, or consensus)
  • Everyone commits 100%‚Äîno exceptions
  • Disagreement stops being voiced publicly
  • The team speaks with one voice externally

üìä Why This Works

Research shows people need two things to commit:

  1. Voice: They need to feel heard. If you didn't listen to their concerns, they won't commit.
  2. Clarity: They need to know a final decision was made. Ambiguity kills commitment.

"Disagree & Commit" provides both: you get voice during debate, and clarity at decision time.

üí° Key Takeaways

  • Consensus is not required for commitment‚Äîvoice and clarity are
  • Supporting a decision you disagree with is leadership, not weakness
  • The debate phase must be real; if people don't feel heard, they won't commit

‚ú® Practice Exercise

This week: In your next team decision, explicitly invoke "Disagree & Commit":

  1. Announce: "We're going to debate this fully, then make a decision and commit."
  2. Encourage dissent during debate
  3. Make the decision clear
  4. Ask: "Can everyone commit to this, even if you disagree?"

The Spar-&-Solve Technique
Start

üéØ What You'll Learn

  • How to use physical warm-ups to prepare for difficult conversations
  • The BJJ-inspired "Spar-&-Solve" conflict technique
  • Why embodied learning improves conflict skills

üìñ Core Concept

In BJJ, you don't learn techniques by reading about them. You learn by doing‚Äîby sparring, making mistakes, adjusting in real-time. Your body learns faster than your mind.

The same applies to conflict. Reading about productive conflict helps, but embodied practice creates lasting change.

ü•ã The Spar-&-Solve Framework

Step 1: Physical Warm-Up (2 minutes)

Before a difficult conversation, do light movement:

  • Shake out your hands and shoulders
  • Take 5 deep breaths
  • Roll your neck and shoulders

Why? This activates your parasympathetic nervous system, counteracting the stress response.

Step 2: Spar (The Conflict)

Enter the conversation with the mindset of a sparring partner:

  • You're both trying to find the best solution, not "win"
  • Resistance isn't personal‚Äîit's data
  • Tension is productive, not destructive

Step 3: Solve (The Resolution)

After exploring all positions, make a clear decision:

  • What did we learn from the spar?
  • What's the best path forward?
  • Who commits to what?

üìä The Neuroscience of Embodied Learning

Studies show that adding physical movement to learning improves retention by 30-50%. When you practice conflict skills with your body (not just your mind), the neural pathways strengthen faster.

BJJ practitioners develop conflict tolerance not through theory, but through thousands of uncomfortable sparring rounds. You can apply the same principle to workplace conflict.

üí° Key Takeaways

  • Physical warm-ups before conflict activate your nervous system in helpful ways
  • Framing conflict as "sparring" changes the emotional tone from threatening to collaborative
  • Embodied practice creates faster, deeper learning than theory alone

‚ú® Practice Exercise

This week: Before your next difficult conversation:

  1. Do a 2-minute physical warm-up (shake, breathe, stretch)
  2. Enter the conversation thinking: "We're sparring partners, not opponents"
  3. After, reflect: Did the warm-up change how you felt? Did the sparring mindset help?

Managing Conflict Physiology
Start

üéØ What You'll Learn

  • What happens in your body during conflict
  • Techniques to stay regulated under stress
  • How to recognize when you've been triggered

üìñ Core Concept

Conflict triggers your nervous system's threat response. Your body doesn't distinguish between physical danger and social conflict‚Äîboth activate the same fight/flight/freeze mechanisms.

‚ö° The Physiology of Conflict

System What Happens Impact on Behavior
Heart Rate Increases (sometimes to 120+ bpm) Harder to think clearly, easier to react emotionally
Cortisol Stress hormone floods system Narrowed focus, reduced empathy, heightened defensiveness
Prefrontal Cortex Blood flow decreases Executive function impaired (planning, perspective-taking)
Amygdala Becomes more active Emotional reactivity increases, rational thought decreases

üö® Warning Signs You're Triggered

  • Your voice gets louder or sharper
  • You interrupt more than usual
  • You feel a physical sensation (chest tightness, heat in face)
  • You stop listening and start planning your rebuttal
  • You think in absolutes ("always," "never," "everyone")
  • You want to attack the person, not just their idea

üõ†Ô∏è Regulation Techniques

1. The 6-Second Pause

When you feel triggered, pause for 6 seconds before responding. Count slowly: 1... 2... 3... 4... 5... 6. This gives your prefrontal cortex time to re-engage.

2. Tactical Breathing (Box Breathing)

Inhale for 4, hold for 4, exhale for 4, hold for 4. Repeat 3 times. This activates your parasympathetic nervous system and calms your physiology.

3. Name It to Tame It

Silently label your emotion: "I'm feeling defensive right now." This simple act activates your prefrontal cortex and reduces amygdala activation.

4. Take a Break

If your heart rate is above 100 bpm, your ability to engage productively is impaired. Say: "I need a 5-minute break to collect my thoughts."

üí° Key Takeaways

  • Conflict triggers real physiological responses‚Äîthis is biology, not weakness
  • Recognizing your warning signs is the first step to regulation
  • Simple techniques (pausing, breathing, naming) can restore your capacity to think clearly

‚ú® Practice Exercise

This week: During your next conflict moment:

  1. Notice your warning signs (which ones from the list above do you experience?)
  2. Use one regulation technique (6-second pause, box breathing, or naming)
  3. After, reflect: Did you notice a shift in your ability to engage?

Expressing Disagreement Effectively
Start

üéØ What You'll Learn

  • Language patterns that invite debate vs. shut it down
  • How to disagree without attacking
  • The difference between positional and interest-based conflict

üìñ Core Concept

How you express disagreement matters as much as what you disagree about. The wrong language makes people defensive. The right language keeps them open.

‚ùå vs ‚úÖ Language Patterns

Instead of... Try...
"That won't work." "I'm concerned about X. How would we address that?"
"You're wrong about that." "I see it differently. Can I share my perspective?"
"We already tried that." "When we tried something similar, we learned X. How is this different?"
"That's a bad idea." "I'm not convinced yet. What problem does this solve?"
"You don't understand..." "Let me share what I'm seeing, then I'd love your reaction."

üîë The "Yes, And..." Principle

Borrowed from improv: acknowledge the other person's point before adding your own. This reduces defensiveness.

Example: "Yes, speed is important, and I want to make sure we're also considering quality. How do we balance both?"

üéØ Positional vs. Interest-Based Conflict

Positional: Arguing over solutions ("We should do X." "No, we should do Y.")

Interest-Based: Exploring underlying needs ("What problem are we trying to solve?")

Example:

  • Position: "We need to hire 3 more engineers."
  • Interest: "We're overwhelmed by the backlog and missing deadlines."

Once you understand the interest (fixing the backlog problem), you can explore multiple solutions: hiring, reducing scope, improving process, etc.

üìä The Power of Curiosity

Research from Harvard's Program on Negotiation shows that asking questions reduces defensiveness and increases openness to alternative solutions.

Instead of asserting your position, ask:

  • "Help me understand your thinking..."
  • "What would success look like from your perspective?"
  • "What concerns you most about my approach?"
  • "What data are you seeing that I'm not?"

üí° Key Takeaways

  • How you disagree matters as much as what you disagree about
  • Language that acknowledges before adding ("Yes, and...") reduces defensiveness
  • Focus on interests (underlying needs) not positions (specific solutions)

‚ú® Practice Exercise

This week: When you disagree with someone:

  1. Catch yourself using "shutdown" language from the left column
  2. Reframe using the right column patterns
  3. Ask at least one curiosity question before stating your position

Track: How did the conversation change?
